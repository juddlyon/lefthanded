<p><strong>Should a left-handed person learn guitar left or right-handed?</strong> Most lefties should learn left-handed. Guitar is an asymmetrical instrument, and the fretting hand does the more demanding work — handing that job to your dominant hand is the natural starting point.</p>

<p>The exceptions: if you've already made real progress right-handed, or you live somewhere lefty instruments are genuinely hard to find. Otherwise, default to lefty.</p>

<h2 id="the-default-case-learn-left-handed">The default case: learn left-handed</h2>

<p>The fretting hand decides 80% of what makes a player sound good — chord shapes, finger independence, vibrato, bends, hammer-ons, pull-offs. The strumming hand handles rhythm and dynamics, which matter, but not in the same fine-motor way.</p>

<p>For a lefty, the dominant hand is the left hand. On a left-handed guitar, that's the strumming hand. On a right-handed guitar, that's the fretting hand. So the question is: do you want your dominant hand handling rhythm and feel, or fingering and technique?</p>

<p>Most teachers and most working players say technique. That's the case for left-handed.</p>

<h2 id="the-counterarguments">The counterarguments (they're weaker than they sound)</h2>

<p>You'll hear three reasons to learn right-handed instead. They're all real, and they're all smaller than people make them out to be.</p>

<p><strong>"Lefty guitars are hard to find."</strong> Twenty years ago, true. Today, Fender, Gibson, Epiphone, Ibanez, PRS, Squier, Taylor, and Martin all run left-handed lines. Selection is thinner — maybe one lefty model for every five right-handed — but quality covers the full range from beginner to studio. See our guide to the <a href="/best-left-handed-guitars/">best left-handed guitars</a> for current picks.</p>

<p><strong>"You won't be able to play other people's guitars."</strong> True, and overrated. You're learning an instrument, not preparing for a guitar-store cocktail party. The guitar in your hands at home is the one you'll spend 99% of your practice time on.</p>

<p><strong>"Some great players learned right-handed."</strong> Also true — Mark Knopfler, Gary Moore, Steve Morse, and others are lefties who play right-handed. But that's selection bias. You don't hear about the lefties who tried right-handed, plateaued, and quit. The argument cuts both ways.</p>

<h2 id="when-right-handed-actually-makes-sense">When right-handed actually makes sense</h2>

<p>There are two scenarios where switching is the right call.</p>

<p><strong>You've already started.</strong> If you've been playing right-handed for six months or more and you're making progress, don't switch. The motor learning you've built is real, and rebuilding it from zero costs more than the marginal benefit of being left-handed-correct.</p>

<p><strong>You live somewhere with no lefty inventory.</strong> If you're in a small town and the nearest left-handed instrument is a six-hour drive away — and you can't easily order online for whatever reason — start right-handed and don't lose sleep over it.</p>

<p>That's the list. "I want to play my friend's guitar at parties" doesn't make it.</p>

<h2 id="what-about-ambidextrous-players">What about ambidextrous and mixed-handed players?</h2>

<p>True ambidexterity is rare — about 1% of people. Mixed-handedness (using different hands for different tasks) is more common, around 9 to 10% of the population. For more on the distinctions, see our piece on <a href="/left-handed-statistics/">left-handedness statistics</a>.</p>

<p>If you're genuinely mixed-handed, try both. Hold a guitar each way for 30 minutes, strum a simple pattern, fret a basic chord shape. One will feel obviously more natural. Trust that.</p>

<h2 id="what-the-famous-lefties-actually-did">What the famous lefties actually did</h2>

<p>The headline players took different paths:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Jimi Hendrix</strong> — left-handed, played a right-handed Stratocaster flipped upside down and restrung. Iconic, but mostly because he was Hendrix, not because the setup was sensible.</li>
<li><strong>Paul McCartney</strong> — left-handed, played a true left-handed bass (Höfner Violin) from the start.</li>
<li><strong>Kurt Cobain</strong> — left-handed, played true left-handed Fender Mustangs and Jaguars.</li>
<li><strong>Tony Iommi</strong> — left-handed, played custom left-handed SGs his entire career.</li>
<li><strong>Albert King</strong> — left-handed, played a right-handed Flying V upside-down without restringing, putting the high strings on top. Singular technique, do not copy.</li>
</ul>

<p>Of the five most-cited left-handed guitar legends, four played true left-handed instruments. The pattern is clear.</p>

<h2 id="how-to-decide-if-youre-starting-from-zero">How to decide if you're starting from zero</h2>

<p>Three questions:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Which hand do you write with?</strong> Left → start with a left-handed guitar.</li>
<li><strong>Have you played a stringed instrument before?</strong> Yes → keep that hand orientation if it worked.</li>
<li><strong>Can you order a lefty model from your country?</strong> Yes → there's no friction worth optimizing around.</li>
</ol>

<p>If you're brand new, lefty, and have internet access — buy a left-handed guitar. The decision isn't close.</p>

<h2 id="frequently-asked-questions">Frequently asked questions</h2>

<h3 id="is-it-easier-to-learn-guitar-left-handed-if-im-a-lefty">Is it easier to learn guitar left-handed if I'm a lefty?</h3>

<p>Yes, in most cases. Your dominant hand handles fretting, which is the more technically demanding job. Strumming with your weaker hand is a smaller hill to climb than fretting with it would be.</p>

<h3 id="can-i-just-flip-a-right-handed-guitar">Can I just flip a right-handed guitar over and play it lefty?</h3>

<p>You can, but it's a poor compromise. The strings will be in the wrong order, the nut and saddle aren't cut for reversed string gauges, and the controls and cutaway will be on the wrong side. If you're committed to playing left-handed, buy a true left-handed guitar — or at minimum, restring a right-handed one and have the nut recut.</p>

<h3 id="why-did-jimi-hendrix-flip-his-guitar">Why did Jimi Hendrix flip his guitar instead of buying a lefty?</h3>

<p>Mostly availability. Left-handed Stratocasters were rare and expensive in the 1960s. Hendrix took a standard right-handed Strat, flipped it, and restrung it for left-handed play. The accidental upside-down position of the controls and tremolo bar became part of his sound — but he was famously reaching across the body to use them. Easier today is easier today.</p>

<h3 id="will-i-be-limited-as-a-lefty-guitarist">Will I be limited as a lefty guitarist?</h3>

<p>No. Selection is narrower than it is for right-handers, but every major brand makes left-handed models, lefty pedals are identical to righty ones, and effects software is hand-agnostic. The only real friction is when you visit a guitar store and most of the wall isn't for you.</p>

<h3 id="should-my-left-handed-child-learn-guitar-left-or-right-handed">Should my left-handed child learn guitar left or right-handed?</h3>

<p>Left-handed. The same logic applies, plus children pick up motor patterns faster and rebuild them harder later. Don't make a kid learn right-handed because you think it'll be more convenient — see our piece on <a href="/should-i-correct-my-left-handed-child/">whether to correct a left-handed child</a> for the broader version of this argument.</p>

<p><strong>What is a left-handed door?</strong> A left-handed door (or left-hand door) has its hinges on the left side when viewed from the outside. When you stand outside and pull the door toward you, it swings to your left, with the handle on the right side of the door.</p>

<p>Door handedness refers to the swing direction and hinge placement, not whether left-handed people can use it. Understanding door handedness is essential when buying replacement doors, locks, and hardware.</p>

<h2>How to determine if a door is left or right-handed</h2>

<p>Follow these steps to identify your door's handedness:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Stand on the outside of the door</strong> - For entry doors, stand outside the building. For interior doors, stand on the side where the door swings away from you (typically the hallway side)</li>
<li><strong>Look at which side has the hinges</strong></li>
<li><strong>Hinges on the left = left-hand door</strong></li>
<li><strong>Hinges on the right = right-hand door</strong></li>
</ol>

<p>An easy memory trick: Stand outside the door facing it, and the hinges tell you the hand. Left hinges = left-hand door. This method works consistently across all door types and manufacturers.</p>

<h2>Left-hand door vs. left-hand reverse door</h2>

<p>Door terminology includes an additional distinction for doors that swing outward rather than inward:</p>

<h3>Left-hand door (LH)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Hinges on the left when viewed from outside</li>
<li>Door swings inward (toward you when standing outside)</li>
<li>Handle is on the right side</li>
<li>Most common for interior doors and many exterior doors</li>
</ul>

<h3>Left-hand reverse door (LHR)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Hinges on the left when viewed from outside</li>
<li>Door swings outward (away from you when standing outside)</li>
<li>Handle is on the right side</li>
<li>Common for exterior doors in hurricane zones, commercial buildings, and screen doors</li>
</ul>

<p>The "reverse" indicates the door swings in the opposite direction of a standard left-hand door. This distinction is critical when ordering doors and hardware, as using the wrong configuration will result in improper installation.</p>

<h2>Why door handedness matters</h2>

<p>Knowing your door's handedness is essential in several situations:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Buying a new door:</strong> Pre-hung doors come in specific handedness configurations and cannot be easily reversed after manufacture</li>
<li><strong>Ordering door hardware:</strong> Some handles, levers, and locks are hand-specific, particularly lever handles that can only be mounted one direction</li>
<li><strong>Installing screen or storm doors:</strong> Must match the primary door's swing direction to avoid conflicts</li>
<li><strong>Replacing hinges:</strong> Hinge placement depends on door hand, and some hinges are handed</li>
<li><strong>Meeting building codes:</strong> Fire exits and commercial doors have specific swing requirements for safety</li>
<li><strong>Planning room layouts:</strong> Door swing affects furniture placement and traffic flow</li>
</ul>

<h2>Common situations requiring door hand identification</h2>

<h3>Entry doors</h3>

<p>Exterior entry doors commonly come as left-hand or right-hand, with reverse options for outward-swinging doors. Before ordering a replacement entry door, carefully note your current door's hand by standing outside and checking the hinge position.</p>

<p>Entry door handedness also affects:</p>
<ul>
<li>Deadbolt installation and orientation</li>
<li>Smart lock compatibility</li>
<li>Weather stripping placement</li>
<li>Threshold drainage direction</li>
</ul>

<h3>Interior doors</h3>

<p>Interior doors in homes are typically inswing (opening into the room). The hand depends on which room is considered the "outside," usually the hallway or common space side. Interior doors typically swing into bedrooms, bathrooms, and closets rather than out into hallways.</p>

<p>When replacing interior doors, consider:</p>
<ul>
<li>Which way the original door swung</li>
<li>Light switch placement (door shouldn't block switches when open)</li>
<li>Furniture and traffic patterns</li>
<li>Privacy needs (bathroom doors typically open inward for privacy)</li>
</ul>

<h3>French doors</h3>

<p>French door pairs include one left-hand and one right-hand door that meet in the middle. When ordering, you'll specify which is the active (primary) door that opens first, while the other door (inactive) has flush bolts at top and bottom.</p>

<h3>Sliding doors</h3>

<p>Sliding doors use different terminology. "Left-hand sliding" typically means the left panel (when viewed from inside) slides in front of the right panel. This is also called "OX" configuration. "Right-hand sliding" (XO) means the right panel slides.</p>

<h2>Does door handedness relate to being left-handed?</h2>

<p>Door handedness has nothing to do with human left-handedness. A left-handed person doesn't need a left-hand door. The terminology simply describes the door's physical configuration and has no connection to human hand preference.</p>

<p>That said, door handle placement can affect ease of use depending on approach direction. If you approach a door from a certain angle, your dominant hand may be more convenient for operating the handle. But this varies by approach angle and personal preference, not door handedness matching human handedness.</p>

<h2>Buying the correct door hardware</h2>

<p>When purchasing door hardware, you'll need to know your door's hand for these items:</p>

<h3>Lever handles</h3>
<p>Many lever handles are "handed" because the lever can only point in one direction. A left-hand door needs a handle that points to the left when installed. Some manufacturers offer "reversible" levers that can be flipped for either hand.</p>

<h3>Deadbolts and locks</h3>
<p>Most modern deadbolts are universal and work on either hand door. However, some electronic locks and smart locks have specific orientations. Check the product specifications before purchasing.</p>

<h3>Door closers</h3>
<p>Commercial door closers must be mounted differently for left-hand versus right-hand doors. The closer arm's position depends on the swing direction.</p>

<h2>Frequently asked questions</h2>

<h3>How do I know if my door is left or right-handed?</h3>
<p>Stand on the outside of the door (exterior side for entry doors, hallway side for interior doors). If the hinges are on the left, it's a left-hand door. If hinges are on the right, it's a right-hand door. This method works for all door types.</p>

<h3>What does "left-hand reverse" door mean?</h3>
<p>A left-hand reverse (LHR) door has hinges on the left side but swings outward (away from you when standing outside) instead of inward. This is common for exterior doors in storm-prone areas, commercial exits, and screen doors.</p>

<h3>Do I need a left-hand or right-hand door lock?</h3>
<p>Most modern door locks are reversible and work on both left and right-hand doors. Check the product specifications before purchasing. Some lever handles and specialty locks may be hand-specific.</p>

<h3>Can I change a door from left-hand to right-hand?</h3>
<p>Changing door handedness requires moving the hinges and strike plate to the opposite side of the frame, filling and refinishing the old hinge and strike locations, and potentially repositioning the door stop. It's usually easier and less expensive to buy a door with the correct hand.</p>

<h3>Why are some exterior doors left-hand reverse?</h3>
<p>Outward-swinging doors (LHR or RHR) are required by building codes for some commercial exits because they're easier to push open during emergencies. They're also preferred in hurricane zones because inward-opening doors can be blown open by wind pressure, while outward-opening doors seal tighter against the frame.</p>

<h3>What is the most common door hand for residential homes?</h3>
<p>There's no universal standard. Door hand depends on the room layout, traffic flow, and architect's preference. Front doors are often right-hand in the United States, but this varies regionally and by home design.</p>

<p><strong>Do I need a left-handed or right-handed bow?</strong> Choose based on your dominant eye, not your dominant hand.</p><p>If your left eye is dominant, use a left-handed bow (held in the right hand, drawn with the left). If your right eye is dominant, use a right-handed bow — even if you are <a href="../left-handed-101-the-definitive-guide/index.html">left-handed</a>.</p><p>We must first understand eye dominance to determine whether we need a left-handed or right-handed bow.</p><p>Eye dominance plays a pivotal role in archery because it affects where we focus when aiming. Archers should aim with their dominant eye for better accuracy; thus, the type of bow we need correlates with this dominant eye.</p><p>A right-handed bow is typically the preferred choice if our right eye is dominant. This allows us to hold the bow with our left hand and draw the string with our right.</p><p>Conversely, we generally fare better with a left-handed bow if our left eye is dominant. This way, we can draw the string with our left hand while the right stabilizes the bow.</p><h2 id="understanding-bow-orientation">Understanding bow orientation</h2><p>When selecting a bow, we must understand the physical differences and how our handedness and eye dominance affect our choice.</p><h3 id="physical-differences-in-bows">Physical differences in bows</h3><p>Bows are designed to be held in one hand while the other draws the string.</p><p><strong>Left-handed bows</strong> are held with the right hand and drawn with the left, while <strong>right-handed bows</strong> are held with the left hand and drawn with the right.</p><p>Key physical components that differ based on orientation include:</p><ul><li><strong>Riser</strong>: The grip and cutout are shaped for comfort and accuracy for the corresponding hand.</li><li><strong>Limbs</strong>: These flex when drawing the bow and can have asymmetric designs to accommodate the draw direction.</li></ul>

<h3 id="handedness-and-eye-dominance">Handedness and eye dominance</h3><p>We must consider our dominant hand and eye when choosing a bow. The dominant hand typically has more strength and skill, which is crucial for drawing the bowstring.</p><p>Eye dominance is vital for aiming.</p><p>To determine eye dominance, extend your arms forward and form a small triangle with your thumbs and forefingers. With both eyes open, look at an object in the distance through the triangle, then alternately close each eye. The eye that keeps the object centered is your dominant eye.</p><p><a href="../why-are-people-left-handed/index.html"><em>Handedness and eye dominance</em></a><em> do not always align</em>. If you are right-handed but left-eye dominant, you might prefer a left-handed bow to take advantage of your dominant eye for aiming. The following list details what to consider:</p><ul><li>Dominant Hand: Use it to draw the bowstring for power and control.</li><li>Eye Dominance: Use it for precise aiming and accuracy.</li><li>Alignment of Hand and Eye Dominance: It may require training or equipment adaptation if mismatched.</li></ul><h2 id="determining-your-dominant-eye">Determining your dominant eye</h2><p>When choosing a bow, it's crucial to understand which of your eyes is dominant. You rely on your dominant eye for accurate aiming.</p><h3 id="eye-dominance-tests">Eye dominance tests</h3><p><strong>The Point Test</strong>: Extend one of your arms and point at a distant object with your index finger. With both eyes open, align your finger with the object. Close one eye at a time. Your dominant eye is the one with which your finger remains aligned with the object.</p><ul><li><strong>Step 1</strong>: Extend your arm and point your finger.</li><li><strong>Step 2</strong>: Align your finger with a distant object.</li><li><strong>Step 3</strong>: Close one eye, then the other.</li><li><strong>Conclusion</strong>: Your dominant eye is the eye that keeps the object aligned with your finger.</li></ul><p><strong>The Miles Test</strong>: Create a small opening between your hands at arm's length and look through it at a distant object. When you close one eye, the object stays in view or moves out of the small opening. The eye that keeps the object in view is your dominant eye.</p><ul><li><strong>Step 1</strong>: Create a small opening with your hands.</li><li><strong>Step 2</strong>: Bring your hands to your eyes while focusing on a distant object.</li><li><strong>Step 3</strong>: Close one eye, then the other.</li><li><strong>Conclusion</strong>: The eye that keeps the object in the opening is your dominant eye.</li></ul><p><strong>The Triangle Test</strong>: Extend both hands at arm's length and overlap your thumbs and forefingers to form a triangle. Focus on a distant object through the triangle. Bring the triangle to your eye while maintaining focus on the object. The eye you bring the triangle to is your dominant eye.</p><ul><li><strong>Step 1</strong>: Form a triangle with your hands.</li><li><strong>Step 2</strong>: Look at a distant object through the triangle.</li><li><strong>Step 3</strong>: Bring the triangle to your eye while keeping the object in view.</li><li><strong>Conclusion</strong>: The eye the triangle naturally goes to is your dominant eye.</li></ul><p>When identifying eye dominance, ensure you perform these tests in a safe and controlled environment to prevent misjudgment or injury.</p><h2 id="matching-bow-to-dominance">Matching bow to dominance</h2><p>When selecting a bow, it is crucial to align the bow type with your dominant eye and hand for optimal control and accuracy.</p><h3 id="right-handed-archers">Right-handed archers</h3><p>Right-handed archers typically have a dominant right eye and hand.

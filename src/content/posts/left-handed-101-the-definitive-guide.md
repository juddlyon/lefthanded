<h2 id="why-left-handedness-matters">Why left-handedness matters</h2><p><strong>What does it mean to be left-handed?</strong> About 10% of people preferentially use their left hand for writing, throwing, and fine motor tasks.</p><p>Left-handedness is linked to distinct brain lateralization patterns, certain cognitive strengths, and a set of everyday challenges in a world designed for the right-handed majority.</p><figure class="kg-card kg-image-card kg-card-hascaption"><img src="/content/images/2023/02/why-left-handedness-matters.webp" class="kg-image" alt="stylized photo of person writing in notebook with left hand" loading="lazy" width="512" height="384"><figcaption>Approximately 10% of the world's population is left-handed</figcaption></figure><h2 id="understanding-left-handedness"><strong>Understanding left-handedness</strong></h2><h3 id="definition-of-left-handedness"><strong>Definition of left-handedness</strong></h3><p><strong>Left-handedness is the tendency to use the left hand to perform manual tasks such as writing, drawing, or using tools.</strong> It is more than just a preference, as it is a complex neurological and biological trait that affects not only the hand used for activities but also other parts of the body such as the foot or the eye.</p><h3 id="prevalence-of-left-handedness"><strong>Prevalence of left-handedness</strong></h3><p>Research shows that about 10% of the world's population is left-handed. The prevalence of left-handedness varies across cultures, with left-handedness being more common in Western societies than in Asian societies. For instance, the Latin word "sinister" originally meant "left," "unlucky," or "evil." Many cultures have forced left-handed children to use their right hand or punished them for using their left hand. In recent years, attitudes towards left-handedness have become more positive, and left-handedness is increasingly viewed as a unique and valuable trait.</p><h2 id="biology-and-genetics-of-left-handedness"><strong>Biology and genetics of left-handedness</strong></h2><p>Left-handedness is a complex trait that results from interactions between biology and genetics. Additionally, there is evidence that left-handedness tends to run in families, suggesting that genetics play a role in handedness.</p><h3 id="environmental-factors-and-handedness"><strong>Environmental factors and handedness</strong></h3><p>Environmental factors can also play a role in handedness.
